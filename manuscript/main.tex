\begin{abstract}
Partisan gerrymandering is structurally entrenched. Federal courts
cannot review it after \textit{Rucho v.~Common Cause}, and forensic
metrics can document manipulation only after it has occurred. What
if congressional districts were drawn not by legislators, but by a
fully specified mathematical rule with no human discretion at any
stage?

We propose and test \emph{DualBalance Districting}: a deterministic
algorithm that computes districts as a pure function of state
geometry, census population, and seat count~$N$, with no
discretionary parameters and no political inputs. Every design choice
is pre-committed before any state's data are examined. Each district
carries roughly $1/N$ of the state's people \emph{and} $1/N$ of its
land, weighted equally in the DualBalance Score.

Applied to all 41 multi-seat states with available VTD data, the
algorithm achieves \emph{Karcher}-compliant population balance on 26
states; both deterministic baselines achieve it on zero. Districts
are substantially less compact than enacted plans (median
Polsby-Popper 0.115 vs.\ 0.281), a structural cost of spanning the
full urban-rural gradient. A rotation sensitivity analysis finds the
DualBalance Score stable across anchor angles (cross-state median
$\sigma = 0.010$) while projected seat counts shift by 1--3 seats in
26 of 41 states, identifying seed rotation as a consequential
pre-committed design choice requiring a legislatively specified rule.

This paper demonstrates feasibility and documents the properties of
an apolitical deterministic design. Whether such a rule constitutes
fair representation is a normative question the algorithm cannot
answer; that judgment belongs to legislatures, courts, and voters.
\end{abstract}
